It's 2:17 AM in Bengaluru, and Prathik hasn't moved from his chair in four hours.

The co-working space around him has emptied out. He's still there because his app is live, his users are awake, and something in the stack is quietly dying. Three months ago, he launched. Four million users arrived faster than he'd modelled. Payments flow through UPI without a single rupee of friction. The product is genuinely, demonstrably good.

And right now, none of that matters.

An API call to an AI layer he doesn't own — hosted on Amazon Web Services, in a data centre in Virginia he's never visited — is timing out. Again. The model powering his core feature was built in San Francisco, trained on data from decisions made in quarterly planning meetings he was never invited to. The GPU cluster that runs it sits in a building he couldn't locate on a map. The pricing that just quietly made his unit economics collapse was a line item in a cloud vendor's margin strategy, not a conversation he was part of.

Prathik's product depends on three things he doesn't control: the model, the compute, and the pricing.

He is not failing. That's the uncomfortable part. Prathik is succeeding — by every measure a startup is supposed to care about. And he is still, structurally, at the mercy of infrastructure he cannot see, negotiate with, or replace in anything less than six months of brutal re-engineering.

This is not a story about one founder having a bad night. It is a story playing out across thousands of companies in the world's most populous digital economy.

## The Numbers That Make Everyone Proud — And Distracted

India's digital growth story is genuinely staggering.

Over **900 million Indians** are now connected to the internet. More than **750 million** carry smartphones. The average Indian consumes over **20 GB of mobile data per month** — a figure that would have sounded like science fiction in 2015, when data cost ₹250 per GB. Today it costs roughly ₹8.

Then there is UPI. In December 2025, India processed **21.6 billion UPI transactions in a single month**, representing over ₹27 lakh crore in value. More than **700 million payment events** richoted through a network that costs nearly nothing to use, works on any phone, and reaches everyone from corner tea stalls to multinational corporations. India now handles approximately **46% of the world's real-time digital payments** — more than the United States, United Kingdom, and European Union combined.

India produces approximately **1.5 million engineering graduates per year**. Indian-origin engineers and executives lead Google, Microsoft, IBM, Adobe, and Palo Alto Networks. The IITs post acceptance rates below 1%.

These numbers deserve celebration. But they are also a distraction from a deeper question. Because scale is visible — everyone can see it. Power is structural. And the two are emphatically not the same thing.

## The Stack That Determines Everything

Digital power is not a single asset. It is a layered system — a stack — where each level depends entirely on the one beneath it. Think of it as a building: you can build extraordinary upper floors, but if you don't own the foundation, you are a tenant, not an owner.

The five layers, from base to surface:

**Compute** — the physical GPU clusters, the specialised chips, the hyperscale data centres that make everything else run. This is the layer of physical scarcity and capital intensity.

**Models** — the AI systems trained on those machines. Increasingly, models are infrastructure themselves: embedded in every product, every service, every automated decision.

**Data** — the raw behavioural signal that makes models useful and specific. Not just volume, but specificity — data that reflects how *your* population thinks, spends, transacts, and speaks.

**Talent** — the engineers, researchers, and architects who build and evolve the system.

**Applications** — the consumer-facing products at the surface. What most people mean when they say "digital economy."

Countries don't need to dominate every layer. But they must control the right ones. The higher up the stack a country operates without owning the layers beneath, the more fragile its position becomes — not in ordinary times, but in the moments that matter most.

## What India Owns: The Honest Accounting

**Data — and it's extraordinary.** UPI's 700 million daily transactions aren't merely financial events. They are high-resolution, real-time behavioural maps of how 1.4 billion people earn, spend, save, and move money — patterns that no survey could capture and no other country's dataset can replicate. Add Aadhaar's 2,700 crore annual authentications, the ONDC commerce network, the Ayushman Bharat health records system, and DigiLocker, and what you have is something without precedent: a live digital nervous system of an entire civilisation, updating hundreds of millions of times per day.

This data has genuine strategic value beyond the applications it currently powers. It is the raw material from which the next generation of Indian AI models — tuned for Indian languages, Indian health patterns, Indian agricultural behaviour, Indian financial decision-making — could be built. The operative word is *could*. Whether it actually is depends on choices that haven't been made yet.

**Talent — but with a paradox.** India exports raw intelligence at scale and imports it back, refined and monetised, as finished products. An Indian engineer in Seattle helps train a frontier language model. That model is packaged into a SaaS tool and sold to Indian companies at dollar-denominated subscription prices. The value transformation — the step where raw intelligence becomes leverage — happens outside the country. India provides the talent. Someone else captures the margin.

**Applications — genuinely world-class.** Razorpay and PhonePe have demonstrated the ability to scale to hundreds of millions of users at cost structures that Western markets can't replicate. Freshworks and Zoho have built global enterprise software from Chennai and Hyderabad. This isn't catch-up. India's application-layer engineering is world-class, and the rest of the world knows it.

## What India Doesn't Own: Where the Leverage Lives

**Compute — the most immediate constraint.** NVIDIA controls between 80% and 90% of the global AI chip market. The H100 that powers frontier AI training costs approximately $30,000 per unit. A serious training run for a large language model requires thousands of them running continuously for weeks — total cost ranging from $50 million to well over $500 million, before infrastructure.

India's hyperscale data centre capacity is growing. But it is overwhelmingly owned and operated by Amazon Web Services, Microsoft Azure, and Google Cloud. These three companies collectively capture the majority of India's cloud spending — money that exits the economy the moment it is committed.

When you don't control compute, you don't control cost. You don't control availability. In a world where compute is increasingly the bottleneck on AI capability, you don't control the pace of your own development. Prathik experienced this at 2:17 AM. India is experiencing a version of it every day.

**Models — the deepest and most durable dependency.** GPT-4, Claude, Gemini, Llama — all built outside India, trained on data that skews heavily toward English and Western contexts, controlled by organisations that answer to American regulators, American investors, and American strategic interests. They are extraordinary systems built by exceptional people. They are not India's systems. India does not set their pricing, determine their availability, govern their safety policies, or decide how they respond to queries about Indian politics, Indian history, or Indian cultural edge cases.

**The gap between contribution and capture.** India generates the inputs at every layer — behavioural data, engineering talent, application revenue, market scale. The value transformation — training, refining, pricing — happens outside. An AI model trained partly on Indian data earns no revenue for India when it is deployed at scale. An Indian engineer who builds foundational AI infrastructure in a US company creates economic value that accrues to that company's shareholders, not to India's economic output. The gap between those two columns is where India's strategic opportunity — and vulnerability — lives.

## Why This Matters More Than It Appears

The framing most countries use for digital competitiveness is headcount and revenue: how many tech jobs, how many unicorns, how large is the sector's GDP contribution. These are real metrics. They measure the right things for the past twenty years.

For the next twenty, a different set of constraints will bind. The organisations that control frontier AI model capabilities will have disproportionate influence over: what information people have access to, how automated decisions about credit, health, and employment are made, what the defaults are when a billion people ask a question, and which languages and cultures AI systems understand well versus poorly.

India's 1.4 billion people interact with systems whose values, biases, and defaults were set by a handful of organisations in San Francisco and Seattle. That's not a conspiracy — it's just how defaults work. And defaults, at scale and over time, shape policy, shape expectations, and shape what possibilities people can even imagine.

Building sovereign capability in compute and models is hard, expensive, and requires sustained political will across election cycles. But the alternative — permanent dependence on infrastructure that India neither controls nor influences — is not a stable equilibrium. It's a slow accumulation of leverage held elsewhere.

The window to change that is open. It won't stay open indefinitely.
